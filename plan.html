Source Imaging Datasets
✓ Freely available — no account required
MGH 100 µm Ex Vivo Brain (Edlow et al., 2019)
Primary anatomical reference · Highest resolution whole-brain MRI dataset in existence

The definitive reference for your ventricular geometry. A post-mortem human brain scanned at 7T with 100 µm isotropic resolution — 1,000× more detailed than a standard clinical MRI. Reveals ventricular walls, aqueduct, cisterns, and foraminal openings with unprecedented clarity. Released for educational and investigational use with no restrictions beyond citation.

Resolution100 µm isotropic (also available at 200 µm and 500 µm for lighter workflows)
CoverageWhole brain, ex vivo (no spinal cord)
FormatNIfTI (.nii.gz), also in MNI stereotactic space
Size~2 TB per flip angle (synthesised FLASH25 volume is much smaller)
Best useManual segmentation reference for foramina, cisterns, aqueduct, and 4th ventricle outlets
⬇ Download — OpenNeuro (ds002179) ⬇ Download — Dryad 📄 Paper (Open Access)
✓ Freely available — FTP / EBRAINS
BigBrain (Amunts et al., 2013)
Histological reference · 20 µm cell-body stained 3D reconstruction

A 3D reconstruction of 7,404 histological sections at 20 µm resolution — close to individual neuron size. Useful as a supplementary anatomical reference for identifying tissue boundaries adjacent to CSF spaces, and for cross-validating ventricular and cisternal geometry. Available under a non-commercial research/education licence; accessible to anyone without institutional login.

Resolution20 µm histological; volumetric downloads at 100, 200, 300, 400 µm (MNI-registered)
FormatNIfTI and MINC; surfaces in STL, gii, WaveFront-OBJ
LicenceNon-commercial research & education use — free to use for your project
Best useSupplementary cross-reference; co-registration with MGH 100 µm data
🌐 BigBrain Project ⬇ Download — EBRAINS 📄 Paper (2013)
✓ Freely available — Zenodo
Spine Generic Dataset + PAM50 Template (Cohen-Adad et al., 2021)
Spinal cord reference · 260-subject multi-site MRI dataset with PAM50 atlas

The standard open-access dataset for spinal cord MRI, collected across 42 centres worldwide. Includes the PAM50 template, which is a standardised spinal cord + spinal canal atlas from C1 to S5. This is the backbone for your spinal SAS geometry — the canal segmentation minus the cord segmentation gives you the spinal subarachnoid space as a 3D volume. Full T1w, T2w, diffusion, and magnetization transfer protocols included.

Subjects260 healthy adults across GE, Philips, Siemens (42 centres)
AtlasPAM50 template includes spinal cord + CSF canal segmentations from C1 to S5
FormatNIfTI (BIDS-organised); processed via Spinal Cord Toolbox
LicenceFully open access — no institutional requirement
⬇ Single Subject — Zenodo ⬇ Multi-Subject — GitHub 📄 Paper (Open Access)
✓ Freely available — Scientific Data
Lumbosacral MRI Dataset — Enhanced Nerve Root Resolution (2024)
Spinal SAS lower region · CISS sequence with CSF & nerve root segmentation

14 healthy adults imaged with CISS, DESS, and T2-TSE sequences specifically designed to highlight the cauda equina and lumbosacral nerve roots within the spinal subarachnoid space. Includes manual annotations of dura, CSF, and nerve roots from L1–S2 — precisely the anatomy that is hardest to recover from the spine generic dataset. Published with 3D Slicer segmentations and Blender models.

CoverageLumbosacral spine (L1–S2), spinal SAS, nerve roots, dura
SequencesCISS, DESS, T2-TSE — CSF-optimised
LicenceOpen access (Scientific Data / CC licence)
📄 Paper + Data Download 💻 GitHub (Blender scripts)

Automated Segmentation Tools (ML)
⚠️
Important: what ML can and cannot do for this task Existing models automate the large CSF spaces well (ventricles, bulk subarachnoid CSF, spinal canal). None currently segment the foramina, cisterns, aqueduct, or small connecting channels automatically. Plan for significant manual work in Phase 3 for those structures.
ML Tool — Brain
SynthSeg 2.0 (FreeSurfer)
Contrast-agnostic whole-brain segmentation · Best automation for ventricular system

The strongest automated segmentation tool for brain CSF spaces. Segments lateral ventricles, 3rd ventricle, 4th ventricle, and extraventricular CSF (label 24 in the aseg atlas) from MRI of any contrast or resolution without retraining. Integrated directly into FreeSurfer — just one command. Run it on the MGH 100 µm dataset (at reduced resolution) or on your own in vivo T1 scan as a starting geometry.

What it segmentsLateral ventricles (L+R), 3rd ventricle, 4th ventricle, choroid plexus, extraventricular CSF, cortical parcellation
What it missesCerebral aqueduct, foramina of Luschka/Magendie, individual basal cisterns, spinal cord
InputAny MRI scan (.nii/.nii.gz/.mgz), any contrast, any resolution
InstallBundled in FreeSurfer ≥7.3 (free, Linux/macOS/Windows via WSL)
💻 GitHub (code + model) 📖 FreeSurfer Wiki 📄 Paper
Run command: mri_synthseg --i input.nii.gz --o seg.nii.gz --parc --robust --vol volumes.csv
ML Tool — Spine
TotalSpineSeg (NeuroPoly Lab, 2024)
Full spinal column + canal segmentation · Derives spinal SAS automatically

nnU-Net based pipeline for automatic instance segmentation of all vertebrae, intervertebral discs, spinal cord, and spinal canal in MRI. Critical for your project: it outputs both a spinal cord mask AND a spinal canal mask separately — the space between them is your spinal subarachnoid space. Works on arbitrary MRI contrasts and orientations.

What it segmentsAll vertebrae (C1–sacrum), IVDs, spinal cord, spinal canal (= dura boundary)
Spinal SASComputed as: spinal canal mask minus spinal cord mask
Installpip install totalspineseg nnunetv2
💻 GitHub 📦 PyPI 📄 Paper / Zenodo
ML Tool — Spine (complementary)
Spinal Cord Toolbox (SCT) v6+
Full spinal cord analysis suite · PAM50 atlas registration · Validated CSA measurements

Comprehensive open-source suite for spinal cord MRI processing. Includes validated deep learning segmentation of the cord and canal, registration to the PAM50 template, and white/grey matter atlas labelling. Use alongside TotalSpineSeg — SCT provides more rigorously validated cross-sectional area measurements and atlas-based level labelling from C1 down to the conus.

Key featuresCord segmentation, canal segmentation, PAM50 registration, level labelling, CSA metrics
InstallConda/pip — all platforms. See documentation.
🌐 Official Site + Docs 💻 GitHub
ML Framework — Custom Training
nnU-Net (Isensee et al., 2021)
Self-configuring segmentation framework · Use to train custom models on your corrected labels

The gold standard framework for training custom medical image segmentation models. After you produce manually corrected segmentations in Phase 3, nnU-Net lets you train a new model on those labels — creating a reusable, subject-agnostic CSF segmentation model that could be a genuine contribution to the field. It automatically configures architecture, patch size, and training strategy from your dataset. No ML expertise required beyond running the scripts.

Use case (here)Train on your manually corrected CSF+foramina labels to produce a reusable model
Installpip install nnunetv2
💻 GitHub 📄 Paper (Nature Methods)

Manual Segmentation & Refinement
⚠️
This phase is unavoidable — and is the core scientific contribution of your project No existing ML model segments the cerebral aqueduct, foramen of Monro, foramina of Luschka, foramen of Magendie, or individual basal cisterns. These must be manually delineated using the MGH 100 µm dataset as your anatomical reference. Plan for significant time investment here; this is also what makes the resulting model novel.

The structures requiring manual work, in order of difficulty:

Structure Difficulty Notes
Lateral ventricles (L + R) Low — SynthSeg handles this Verify and correct SynthSeg output in 3D Slicer
3rd ventricle Low — SynthSeg handles this Thin structure; verify lateral walls carefully
Cerebral aqueduct (of Sylvius) Medium — manual required ~1.5 mm diameter; visible at 100 µm but needs careful slice-by-slice tracing
4th ventricle Medium — SynthSeg partial Complex tent-shaped geometry; verify lateral recesses
Foramen of Magendie (median aperture) Medium — manual required Midline opening from 4th ventricle to cisterna magna; ~5 mm
Foramina of Luschka (lateral apertures, bilateral) Hard — manual required ~2–3 mm; lateral recesses of 4th ventricle; most challenging structure
Foramina of Monro (interventricular, bilateral) Medium — manual required Connects lateral ventricles to 3rd ventricle; visible at 100 µm
Basal cisterns (cisterna magna, prepontine, ambient, etc.) Hard — manual required Ill-defined boundaries; use perimesencephalic anatomy as landmarks
Cerebral subarachnoid space (convexity, sulci) Medium — SynthSeg label 24 + refine Extract from FreeSurfer aseg label 24; refine sulcal depths
Spinal subarachnoid space (cervical to sacral) Low–Medium — TotalSpineSeg Canal − cord = SAS; verify at foramen magnum junction
Foramen magnum junction (cranio-spinal transition) Hard — manual required Critical connection point; brain and spinal datasets must be co-registered and joined here
Segmentation Tool
3D Slicer
Free, open-source medical image analysis and segmentation

The recommended tool for all manual segmentation work. Load the MGH 100 µm NIfTI file, use the Segment Editor module to paint CSF structures slice-by-slice, and export as 3D surface meshes (STL or OBJ). Has built-in surface smoothing, boolean operations, and mesh export. The lumbosacral dataset paper used 3D Slicer v5.4.0 for all its segmentations.

⬇ Download 3D Slicer (free) 📖 Segment Editor Docs
4
Mesh Generation & Model Assembly

Once all segmentation labels are finalised, convert them to 3D surface or volumetric meshes suitable for visualisation, simulation, or further analysis.

Meshing Tool
SVMTK / mri2fem-II
Python · Convert MRI segmentations to finite-element meshes of CSF spaces

Built specifically for this use case: converting MRI-derived brain surface meshes into finite element meshes of the subarachnoid space and ventricular system for computational modelling (e.g., CSF flow simulation using FEniCS). The mri2fem-II book chapter directly demonstrates meshing the SAS + ventricles from a brain surface triangulation. Fully open source.

💻 SVMTK GitHub 📄 mri2fem-II Chapter
Meshing / Visualisation
Blender (free) + MeshLab (free)
Surface mesh cleanup, boolean union, visualisation

Use MeshLab for mesh cleaning (removing self-intersections, decimation, remeshing). Use Blender for final assembly, boolean operations to join brain SAS with ventricular cavities, and high-quality rendering. The lumbosacral spine paper used Blender v4.0.2 for its 3D model construction. Both tools are free and do not require institutional access.

⬇ Blender ⬇ MeshLab
Registration Tool
ANTs (Advanced Normalization Tools)
Co-register brain and spinal datasets at the cranio-spinal junction

Critical for joining your brain-derived CSF geometry with the spinal cord dataset. ANTs provides robust nonlinear registration between different MRI datasets and to standard spaces (MNI, PAM50). Needed specifically to align the 4th ventricle / cisterna magna region with the cervical spinal canal at the foramen magnum. Fully open-source, widely used.

💻 ANTs GitHub 💻 ANTsPy (Python interface)

6
Step-by-Step Workflow
1

Download datasets

Get the MGH 100 µm brain from OpenNeuro (ds002179) — start with the synthesised FLASH25 volume or the 200 µm downsampled version to keep file sizes manageable. Download the Spine Generic single-subject dataset from Zenodo. Optionally download the BigBrain 200 µm volume from EBRAINS for reference.

2

Install tools

Install FreeSurfer ≥7.3 (includes SynthSeg), Spinal Cord Toolbox, and 3D Slicer. Install TotalSpineSeg via pip. Install ANTsPy via pip. All free, all Linux/macOS/Windows (SCT and FreeSurfer work best on Linux or WSL2 on Windows).

3

Run SynthSeg on brain data

Run mri_synthseg on the MGH 100 µm data (resampled to 1 mm first, or run on a companion in vivo T1 scan). Extract ventricles (labels 4, 5, 14, 15, 43, 44) and extraventricular CSF (label 24) from aseg.mgz. This gives you the automated bootstrap for all major ventricular cavities.

4

Run TotalSpineSeg + SCT on spinal data

Run TotalSpineSeg on the Spine Generic T2w image to get cord + canal segmentations. Run SCT's sct_deepseg_sc and sct_register_to_template to register to PAM50. Compute spinal SAS = canal − cord.

5

Manual segmentation in 3D Slicer (Phase 3 — the core work)

Load the MGH 100 µm volume into 3D Slicer. Using the Segment Editor, manually trace: cerebral aqueduct (axial series), foramen of Monro (coronal), 4th ventricle lateral recesses, foramen of Magendie (sagittal midline), and foramina of Luschka (axial oblique). Separately delineate each named cistern. Export as NIfTI label maps.

6

Co-register brain and spinal datasets

Use ANTs to register the brain dataset to MNI space. Register the PAM50 spine to the same MNI space. Join at the foramen magnum: the cisterna magna segmentation from the brain side should seamlessly connect with the C1 spinal canal segmentation from the spine side. This junction requires careful manual verification.

7

Generate meshes

Export all label maps as surface meshes from 3D Slicer (Model Maker module) or using SVMTK. Clean meshes in MeshLab (remove self-intersections, smooth, decimate). Assemble the complete CSF system (ventricles + brain SAS + spinal SAS + connecting channels) as a single watertight mesh in Blender using boolean union operations.

8

(Optional) Train a custom nnU-Net model

Convert your finalised label maps into nnU-Net training format. Train a model on your corrected segmentations — you'll likely need 10–20 labelled scans for reasonable performance. This is an optional but high-value step that would produce a reusable, publicly releasable tool.
